The first useful thing Cerro Negro gave us was not a sample. It was a
warning: the approach looks short on the map and long under a pack.

We started with the kind of confidence that only survives for half a
kilometer. After that, the slope turned loose, the GPS decided every
waypoint was a suggestion, and the outcrop began doing what outcrops do
best: refusing to be the cartoon version of itself.

## Field rhythm

By day two, the work settled into a pattern.

- Walk the contact until the texture changes.
- Photograph the sample in place before touching it.
- Write the boring metadata while the boring metadata is still obvious.
- Bag less material than pride wants and more than the lab will forgive.

The basaltic units were cleaner than expected. The andesitic flows were
not. Some of the best-looking hand samples became the least useful thin
sections, which feels unfair but is probably character-building.

## What I would change

I would build the day around fewer stations and better pauses. The field
notebook gets worse when every stop feels like a delay. A slower
traverse would have given the contacts more room to become
interpretable instead of merely locatable.

There is a design lesson in that too. A data pipeline can record
everything and still fail to preserve the shape of the day. Next round,
I want the field sheet to carry more context: weather, confidence,
sketch quality, and the small decisions that determine whether a sample
is a story or just a coordinate.

## Working hypothesis

The suite still looks like a coherent arc package, but the early
thin-section checks suggest more mixing than the hand samples
advertised. I am keeping the interpretation loose until the pyroxene
chemistry comes back.

The rocks are doing the enjoyable thing: making the simple model
useful, then immediately making it incomplete.
