Some papers become less like papers and more like rooms you keep
walking back into. Putirka (2008) is one of those for me: part
reference, part argument, part reminder that every thermometer has a
personality.

The reread was less about memorizing equations and more about noticing
where the method asks for humility. Calibration datasets are not
background furniture. They are the room.

## Margin notes

What I keep circling:

- Equilibrium tests are not optional decoration.
- Mineral-melt pairing is where the quiet errors enter.
- Reporting uncertainty should feel like part of the result, not an
  apology after it.
- The best-looking temperature can still be the wrong answer if the
  assemblage is lying.

That last point is the one I forget when I am tired. A spreadsheet can
make a number look inevitable. The thin section usually knows better.

## Useful friction

The paper is still useful because it gives you enough rope to build
something real and enough caveats to notice when the rope is around
your ankle.

For the CVZ samples, I am less interested in a single "correct"
pressure than in whether the method produces coherent structure across
texture, mineral chemistry, and field position. Agreement matters, but
interpretable disagreement might matter more.

## Next pass

I want to turn this reread into a small checklist that runs beside the
calculation notebook:

- What phase pair was used?
- What equilibrium filter passed?
- What calibration range is being stretched?
- What sample context argues against the number?

That is the part of the workflow where code and geology should be
talking to each other, not taking turns.
